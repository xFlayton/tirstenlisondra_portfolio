bylte is map-based application aimed at connecting fashion consumers to smaller businesses.

As co-designer I was tasked to create an application that improved a current-issue.

The Problem

People who want unique fashion pieces have a hard time finding small boutiques because most shopping sites and search engines focus on big brands.

Difficulty finding stores carrying a certain product.

Results from 81 fashion-focused participants.

Identifying Gaps within Dominant Platforms

Looking into our direct competition,
I discovered major shortcomings.

Pinterest offers an innovative ‘Shop the Look’ feature, but the curated items often don’t match the products shown in the photos.

Users often get broad results with limited relevance to niche fashion interests, requiring extra effort to find hidden gems.

Larger or chain stores tend to appear more prominently, overshadowing small, independent boutiques.

Ideating A Solution
I conducted a week-long UXR campaign to determine what they would need from a potential solution.
After gathering the results, we came up with a question to guide our design process:
How might different navigational approaches impact user engagement and satisfaction when looking for specialty fashion?
The Process – 1
To start, I decided on 3 key design principles:
1. Simple and visually clean interface prioritizing small-scale retailers.
2. High contrast between text and background for accessibility.
3. Consistent spacing and layout patterns across pages for intuitive navigation.
The Process - 2
To establish cosnsitency across each collaborator,
I created a design system.
The Rationale
The idea of a customizable, tailored
experience really spoke to me using
a range of varying colors rather than
two main contrasting ones.

The final colors are made to be a
muted version of their base colors to
be easier on the eyes of the viewers,
reducing visual clutter, and overall
making the application more user friendly.
The Process - 3
Collecting inspiration from mobile wallets.
I created a wallet-like home screen.

Inspired by mobile wallets, we designed a dynamic, swipeable home screen with both vertical and horizontal views to empower user choice.

Embracing Feedback and Iteration
Staying open minded during the design process allows me to create solutions that truly meet users’ needs rather than clinging to initial concepts.

The wallet concept became redundant once we introduced a bottom navigation bar – I had to let it go.
The Final Product
After the two-week sprint, heres a look at the final product.
Reflecting Back on My Experience
Looking back at the development process, there is always room for improvement.

1.

As the sole UX researcher, I had to use surveys, competitor analysis, and persona development to validate our concept and guide design decisions.

2.

Since we were all designers we faced inconsistencies in a variety of aspects, which required extra time to fix. If I were to do this again, I would implement a stricter design system early and ensure clear ownership for components.

3.

I learned the importance of staying open-minded. Pivoting early after research led us to a more meaningful solution and reinforced that great design comes from adapting to user needs, not initial assumptions.
